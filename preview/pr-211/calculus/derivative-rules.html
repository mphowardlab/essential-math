1.4. Product, quotient, and chain rule#

1.4.1. Product Rule#

If \(f(x) = u(x) v(x)\) then:

\[f' = uv' + vu'\]

“u-dv plus v-du” “first-derivative-of-second plus second-derivative-of-first”

1.4.1.1. Example 1:#

(1.31)#\[\begin{equation} f(x) = (x-3)(x-1) \end{equation}\]

Old way: Expand The Polynomial

(1.32)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= x^2 - 4x + 3 \\ f'(x) &= 2x - 4 \end{align}\]

New way:

(1.33)#\[\begin{align} u &= x-3 & v &= x-1 \\ u' &= 1 & v' &= 1 \end{align}\]

so

(1.34)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = (x-3) \cdot 1 + (x-1) \cdot 1 = 2x - 4 \end{equation}\]

This example is a little silly, but the product rule is a life saver for functions that are hard to expand!

(1.35)#\[\begin{equation} f(x) = \underbrace{(x+1)}_u \cdot \underbrace{(2x^2+5)(5x^3-4)}_v \end{equation}\]
(1.36)#\[\begin{align} u &= x+1 and v &= (2x^2+5)(5x^3-4) \\ u' &= 1 and v' &= ? Product Rule Again! \end{align}\]
(1.37)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = (x+1)v' + (2x^2 +3)(5x^3-4)(1) \end{equation}\]
(1.38)#\[\begin{align} u &= 2x^2+5 and v &= 5x^3-4 \\ u' &= 4x and v' &= 15x^2 \end{align}\]
(1.39)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = (x+1)[(2x^2+3)(15x^2) + (5x^3-4)(4x)] + (2x^2+5)(5x^3-4) \end{equation}\]

…simplify as much as you like

1.4.1.2. Example 2:#

(1.40)#\[\begin{equation} f(x) = \frac{1}{x} e^x \end{equation}\]
(1.41)#\[\begin{align} u &= \frac{1}{x} and v &= e^x \\ u' &= -\frac{1}{x^2} and v' &= e^x \end{align}\]
(1.42)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \frac{1}{x}e^x + e^x(-\frac{1}{x^2}) = e^x(\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{x^2}) \end{equation}\]

1.4.1.3. Example 3:#

(1.43)#\[\begin{equation} f(x) = (x^2+3)\ln(x) \end{equation}\]
(1.44)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2+3 and v &= \ln(x) \\ u' &= 2x and v' = \frac{1}{x} \end{align}\]
(1.45)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = (x^2+3)(\frac{1}{x}) + \ln(x)(2x) = \frac{x^2+3}{x} + 2x\ln(x) \end{equation}\]

1.4.2. Chain Rule#

The chain rule is used to differentiate composite functions, when the “inside” of the function is more complicated than what we know.

Chain rule

If a function \(f(x)\) can be written as \(f(u(x))\), then:

(1.46)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = f'(u) u'(x) \end{equation}\]

To demonstrate this rule, consider \(f(x) = (x-1)^2\). Previously, we would have needed to expand this function before differentiating:

(1.47)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= x^2 - 2x + 1 \\ f'(x) &= 2x - 2 \end{align}\]

With the chain rule, instead we recognize

(1.48)#\[\begin{align} u &= x - 1 & f &= u^2 \\ u' &= 1 & f' &= 2u \end{align}\]

so

(1.49)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = 2u \dd{}{u}{x} = 2(x-1) \cdot 1 = 2x - 2 \end{equation}\]

The results match! Some additional examples:

  1. \(f(x) = e^{x^2}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = x^2\):

    (1.50)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2 & f &= e^u \\ u' &= 2x & f' &= e^u \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.51)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{x^2} \cdot 2x \end{equation}\]

  2. \(f(x) = \ln(1 + 2x)\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.52)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= \ln u \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= \frac{1}{u} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.53)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{1}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{2}{1 + 2x} \end{equation}\]

  3. \(f(x) = \dfrac{2}{1 + 2x}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.54)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= 2u^{-1} \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= -2u^{-2} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.55)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = -2u^{-2} \cdot \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{-4}{(1 + 2x)^2} \end{equation}\]
