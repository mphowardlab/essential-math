Limits

1.2. Limits#

Limit Laws

Constant:

(1.1)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} k = k \end{equation}\]

Identity:

(1.2)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} x = c \end{equation}\]

Continuous Function:

(1.3)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c) \end{equation}\]
This is applicable if f is "continuous" at x (no jumps or holes).

If \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L\) and \(\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = M\) , then

Sum:

(1.4)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} f(x) + g(x) = L + M \end{equation}\]

Difference:

(1.5)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} f(x) - g(x) = L - M \end{equation}\]

Product:

(1.6)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} f(x)g(x) = LM \end{equation}\]

Quotient:

(1.7)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M} \end{equation}\]
This is applicable if $M \neq 0$.

Power:

(1.8)#\[\begin{equation} \lim_{x \to c} f(x)^{\frac{r}{s}} = L^{\frac{r}{s}} \end{equation}\]
'r' and 's' are integers, $s \neq 0$, and $L > 0$ if s is even

Taking Limits

“Nice” functions can be evaluated directly

(1.9)#\[\begin{align} \lim_{x \to -1} x^3 + 4x^2 -3 &= (-1)^3 + 4(-1)^2 -3 \\ &= -1 + 4 -3 \\ &= 0 \end{align}\]

This works even if they are complicated

(1.10)#\[\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 4} \left(\frac{x^2 + 9}{x + 1} \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} &= \left(\frac{4^2 + 9}{4 + 1} \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \\ &= (\frac{25}{5})^(\frac{1}{2}) \\ &= \sqrt{5} \end{align}\]

Limits are most useful when functions have “holes” (not definited at \(x_{\text{0}}\))

(1.11)#\[\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 + x -2}{x^2 - x} &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{(x+2)(x+1)}{x(x+1)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x+2}{x} \\ &= 3 \end{align}\]

Evaluating \(\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 + x -2}{x^2 - x}\) gives \(\frac{0}{0}\). To remove this hole, we can simplify the equation by factoring.

This works even if the factors are “ugly”

(1.12)#\[\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{4x - x^2}{2 - \sqrt{x}} &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{x(4 - x)}{2 - \sqrt{x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{x(2 + \sqrt{x})(2 - \sqrt{x})}{2 - \sqrt{x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 4} x(2 + \sqrt{x}) \\ &= 4(2 + \sqrt{4}) \\ &= 16 \end{align}\]

\(\lim_{x \to 4} \frac{4x - x^2}{2 - \sqrt{x}} = \frac{0}{0}\) so we factor and use difference of squares to remove the hole.

Recall: difference of squares \rightarrow \(a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b)\)

Another useful trick is to multiply by a factor of “1” that makes a difference of squares (multiple by \(\frac{reciprocal}{reciprocal}\))

(1.13)#\[\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{3x + 1} - 1}{x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{(\sqrt{3x + 1} - 1)(\sqrt{3x + 1} + 1)}{x(\sqrt{3x + 1} + 1)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{(\sqrt{3x + 1})^2 - (1)^2}{x(\sqrt{3x + 1} + 1)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{3x + 1 - 1}{x(\sqrt{3x + 1} + 1)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{3}{\sqrt{3x + 1} + 1} \\ &= \frac{3}{2} \end{align}\]
