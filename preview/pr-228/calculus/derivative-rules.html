1.4. Rules for derivatives#

1.4.1. Quotient Rule#

If the quotient rule you wish to know, it’s “low-d-high less high-d-low” then draw the line and down below, denominator squared will go.

Quotient Rule

If a function \(f(x)\) can be written as \(f (x) = \frac{u(x)}{v(x)}\) and \( v (x) \ne 0\), then:

(1.47)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \frac{v u' - u v'}{v^2} \end{equation}\]

To demonstrate this rule, consider \(f(x) = \frac{x-1}{x}\). Previously, we would have needed to separate the numerator before differentiating:

(1.48)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= 1 - \frac{1}{x} \\ f' &= 0 + \frac{1}{x^2} &= \frac{1}{x^2} \end{align}\]

With the qoutient rule, instead we recognize

(1.49)#\[\begin{align} u &= x - 1 & v &= x \\ u' &= 1 & v' &= 1 \end{align}\]

so

(1.50)#\[\begin{equation} f' = \frac{ x \cdot 1 - (x-1) \cdot 1 }{x^2} = \frac{1}{x^2} \end{equation}\]

This was obviously harder than the old way, but there are other cases where it is helpful to do the quotient rule!

  1. \(f(x) = \frac{x^2 -1}{x^4 + 2}\)

    (1.51)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2 -1 & v &= x^4 +2 \\ u' &= 2x & v' =& 4x^3 \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.52)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \frac{ (x^4 + 2) \cdot (2x) - (x^2 - 1) \cdot (4x^3)}{(x^4 +2)^2} = \frac{2x^5 + 4x^2 - 4x^5 +4x^3}{x^8 + 2x^4 + 4} \end{equation}\]

  2. \(f(x) = \frac{e^{x}}{1 + x}\)

(1.53)#\[\begin{align} u &= e^{x} & v &= 1 + x \\ u' &= e^{x} & v' &= 1 \end{align}\]

so

(1.54)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \frac{(1 + x) \cdot e^{x} - e^{x} \cdot 1}{(1 + x)^2} = \frac{x \cdot e^{x}}{(1 + x)^2} \end{equation}\]

  1. \(f(x) = \frac{(x - 1) \cdot (x^2 - 2x)}{x^4}\)

    (1.55)#\[\begin{align} u &= (x -1) \cdot (x^2 -2x) \\ u' &= (x - 1) \cdot (2x-2) \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.56)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \frac{x^4 \cdot (x - 1) \cdot (2x - 2) - (x - 1) \cdot (x^2 -2x) \cdot (4x^3)}{x^8} \end{equation}\]

    v.s

    (1.57)#\[\begin{equation} f(x) = \frac{1}{x^4} \cdot x^3 -3x^2 +2x = x^-1 -3x^-2 +2x^-3 f'(x) = -\frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{6}{x^3} - \frac{6}{x^4} \end{equation}\]

Fastest way depends on the problem!

1.4.3. Chain rule#

The chain rule is used to differentiate composite functions, when the “inside” of the function is more complicated than what we know.

Chain rule

If a function \(f(x)\) can be written as \(f(u(x))\), then:

(1.58)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = f'(u) u'(x) \end{equation}\]

To demonstrate this rule, consider \(f(x) = (x-1)^2\). Previously, we would have needed to expand this function before differentiating:

(1.59)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= x^2 - 2x + 1 \\ f'(x) &= 2x - 2 \end{align}\]

With the chain rule, instead we recognize

(1.60)#\[\begin{align} u &= x - 1 & f &= u^2 \\ u' &= 1 & f' &= 2u \end{align}\]

so

(1.61)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = 2u \dd{}{u}{x} = 2(x-1) \cdot 1 = 2x - 2 \end{equation}\]

The results match! Some additional examples:

  1. \(f(x) = e^{x^2}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = x^2\):

    (1.62)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2 & f &= e^u \\ u' &= 2x & f' &= e^u \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.63)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{x^2} \cdot 2x \end{equation}\]

  2. \(f(x) = \ln(1 + 2x)\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.64)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= \ln u \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= \frac{1}{u} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.65)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{1}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{2}{1 + 2x} \end{equation}\]

  3. \(f(x) = \dfrac{2}{1 + 2x}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.66)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= 2u^{-1} \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= -2u^{-2} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.67)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = -2u^{-2} \cdot \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{-4}{(1 + 2x)^2} \end{equation}\]

All of these follow from knowing only two derivatives: \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\)! For example, to find the derivative of \(\tan x\), use the quotient rule:

(1.68)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= \tan x = \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} \\ f'(x) &= \frac{\cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} \\ &= \frac{\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x}{\cos^2 x}\\ &= \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} \\ &= \sec^2 x \end{align}\]

Or, to find the derivative of \(\csc x\), use the chain rule:

(1.69)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= \csc x = (\sin x)^{-1} \\ f'(x) &= -(\sin x)^{-2} \dd{}{}{x}(\sin x) \\ &= (\sin x)^{-2} \cdot (-\cos x) \\ &= \frac{-1}{\sin x} \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} \\ &= -\csc x \cot x \end{align}\]

1.4.5. Skill builder problems#

  1. \(f(x) = 3 \cos x + \sin x\)

    Solution
    (1.70)#\[\begin{align} f' &= 3 \dd{}{}{x}(\cos x) + 4 \dd{}{}{x}(\sin x)\\ &= -3\sin x + 4\cos x \end{align}\]

  2. \(f(x) = 2 \sin x \cos x\)

    Solution

    Use the product rule with:

    (1.71)#\[\begin{align} u &= \sin x & v &= \cos x\\ u' &= \cos x & v' &= -\sin x \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.72)#\[\begin{align} f' &= 2[\sin x(-\sin x) + \cos x(\cos x)] \\ &= 2(\cos^2 x-\sin^2 x) \\ &= 2 \cos 2x\\ \end{align}\]

  3. \(f(x) = \sin 2x\)

    Solution

    Use the chain rule with

    (1.73)#\[\begin{align} u &= 2x & f &= \sin u \\ u' &= 2 & f' = \cos u \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.74)#\[\begin{equation} f' = \cos{u} \cdot 2 = 2 \cos 2 x \end{equation}\]

  4. \(f(x) = \tan({x^2+2x+3})\)

    Solution

    Use the chain rule with

    (1.75)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2+2x+3 & f = \tan u\\ u' &= 2x+2 & f' = \sec^2 u\\ \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.76)#\[\begin{align} f' &= \sec^2 u \cdot u'\\ &= (2x+2)\sec^2(x^2+2x+3)\\ \end{align}\]

  5. \(f(x) = e^{\cos^2 x}\)

    Solution

    Use the chain rule with

    (1.77)#\[\begin{align} u &= \cos^2 x & f = e^{u}\\ u' &= \dd{}{}{x}(\cos^2 x) & f' = e^{u} \end{align}\]

    So

    (1.78)#\[\begin{equation} f' = e^{u} u' = e^{\cos^2 x} \dd{}{}{x}(\cos^2 x) \end{equation}\]

    Use the chain rule again with

    (1.79)#\[\begin{align} u &= \cos x & f = u^{2}\\ u' &= -\sin x & f' = 2u\\ \end{align}\]

    So

    (1.80)#\[\begin{align} f' &= e^{\cos^2 x}(2uu')\\ &= e^{\cos^2 x}(2\cos x \cdot -\sin x)\\ &= -2 \sin x \cos x e^{\cos^2 x}\\ \end{align}\]
