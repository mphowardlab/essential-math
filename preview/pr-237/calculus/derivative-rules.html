1.4. Product, quotient, and chain rule#

1.4.1. Chain Rule#

The chain rule is used to differentiate composite functions, when the “inside” of the function is more complicated than what we know.

Chain rule

If a function \(f(x)\) can be written as \(f(u(x))\), then:

(1.31)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = f'(u) u'(x) \end{equation}\]

To demonstrate this rule, consider \(f(x) = (x-1)^2\). Previously, we would have needed to expand this function before differentiating:

(1.32)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= x^2 - 2x + 1 \\ f'(x) &= 2x - 2 \end{align}\]

With the chain rule, instead we recognize

(1.33)#\[\begin{align} u &= x - 1 & f &= u^2 \\ u' &= 1 & f' &= 2u \end{align}\]

so

(1.34)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = 2u \dd{}{u}{x} = 2(x-1) \cdot 1 = 2x - 2 \end{equation}\]

The results match! Some additional examples:

  1. \(f(x) = e^{x^2}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = x^2\):

    (1.35)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2 & f &= e^u \\ u' &= 2x & f' &= e^u \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.36)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{x^2} \cdot 2x \end{equation}\]

  2. \(f(x) = \ln(1 + 2x)\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.37)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= \ln u \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= \frac{1}{u} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.38)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{1}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{2}{1 + 2x} \end{equation}\]

  3. \(f(x) = \dfrac{2}{1 + 2x}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.39)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= 2u^{-1} \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= -2u^{-2} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.40)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = -2u^{-2} \cdot \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{-4}{(1 + 2x)^2} \end{equation}\]

All of these follow from knowing only two derivatives: \(\sin(x)\) and \(\cos(x)\)!

\textbf{Proof of derivative of \(\tan(x)\)}

(1.41)#\[\begin{align} f(x) = \tan(x) \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)} \\ u = \sin(x) \; v = \cos(x) \\ u' = \cos(x) \; v' = -\sin(x) \\ f'(x) = \frac{\cos(\cos(x)) - \sin(-\sin(x))}{\cos^2(x)} = \frac{\sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x)}{\cos^2(x)} = \frac{1}{\cos^2(x)} = \sec^2(x) \end{align}\]

\textbf{Proof of derivative of \(\csc(x)\)}

(1.42)#\[\begin{align} f(x) = \csc(x) = (\sin(x))^{-1} \\ u = \sin(x) \; f(u) = u^{-1} \\ u'(x) = \cos(x) \; f'(u) = -u^{-2} \\ f'(x) = -u^{-2} u' = -(\sin(x))^{-2} \cosx = \frac{-1}{\sin(x)} \frac{\cos(x)}{\sin(x)} = -\csc(x) \cot(x) \end{align}\]
