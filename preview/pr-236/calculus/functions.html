1.1. Functions#

1.1.1. 1. What is a function?#

A rule “f” that assigns a \(\textbf{unique}\) value \(f(x)\) to an input \(x\)

(1.1)#\[\begin{align} \text{x: independent variable} \hspace{2cm} \text{domain: all possible x}\\ \text{y: dependent variable} \hspace{2cm} \text{range: all possible y} \end{align}\]

A function can be represented by:

  • An equation: \(f(x) = x^2\)

  • A table:

  • A graph:

  • A description: “square x”

\(\textbf{Not all curves are functions!}\) \(f\) must uniquely map \(x\)

\(x^2 + y^2 = 4\)

\(\textbf{Vertical line test:}\) a vertical line cannot intersect a function twice

1.1.2. 2. Common functions#

1.1.2.1. Linear functions#

(1.2)#\[\begin{align} y = mx + b \text{ ("lines")} \end{align}\]

  • m: slope

  • b: intercept

1.1.2.2. Power functions#

(1.3)#\[\begin{align} y = kx^a \text{ ("power law")} \end{align}\]

  • k: prefactor

  • a: power

1.1.2.3. Polynomials#

(1.4)#\[\begin{align} y = a_n x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_1x + a_0 \end{align}\]

  • n: degree

(1.5)#\[\begin{align} n=1&: \text{linear}\\ n=2&: \text{quadratic}\\ n=3&: \text{cubic} \end{align}\]

1.1.2.4. Exponential#

(1.6)#\[\begin{align} y = a^x \text{ (a > 0, a $\neq$ 1)} \end{align}\]

  • a: base

Special case: a = e (≈ 2.718…) = Euler’s number

1.1.2.5. Logarithmic#

(1.7)#\[\begin{align} y = \log_a x \text{ (a > 0, a $\neq$ 1)} \end{align}\]

Special cases:

(1.8)#\[\begin{align} a = e &\Rightarrow \log_e = \ln = \text{natural logarithm}\\ a = 10 &\Rightarrow \log_{10} = \log = \text{common logarithm} \end{align}\]

1.1.2.6. Trigonometric#

(1.9)#\[\begin{align} \sin x \hspace{1cm} \csc x\\ \cos x \hspace{1cm} \sec x\\ \tan x \hspace{1cm} \cot x \end{align}\]

1.1.3. Manipulating functions#

Horizontal shift

  • \(f(x+a)\) shifts f left by a.

  • \(f(x-a)\) shifts f right by a.

Vertical shift

  • \(f(x)+b\) shifts f up by b.

  • \(f(x)-b\) shifts f down by b.

Horizontal scaling

  • Scaling \(f(cx)\) compresses f horizontally by c.

  • Scaling \(f(x/c)\) stretches f horizontally by c.

Vertical scaling

  • Scaling \(f(x)/c\) compresses f vertically by c.

  • Scaling \(cf(x)\) streches f vertically by c.

1.1.4. Trigonometric functions#

The given unit circle has a triangle drawn within which represents how trigonometric functions are related to lengths and heights of the sides of right triangles.

The circle has a radius and is centered at (0,0). A point on the circle is defined by angle \(\theta\) measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis. Angles can be measured in degrees or radians:

(1.10)#\[\begin{equation} \theta({\rm rad.}) = \theta({\rm deg.}) \left( \frac{\pi}{180^{\circ}} \right) \end{equation}\]

The trigonometric functions describe how the coordinates of a point on the unit circle relate to the angle \(\theta\):

(1.11)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta)& = \frac{y}{r}& \cos(\theta)& = \frac{x}{r}& \tan(\theta)& = \frac{y}{x}\\ \csc(\theta)& = \frac{r}{y}& \sec(\theta)& = \frac{r}{x}& \cot(\theta)& = \frac{x}{y}\\ \end{align}\]

Each quadrant of the unit circle corresponds to a positive trigonometric function: All, Sine, Tangent, and Cosine.

Tip

A mnemonic to remember this order is “All Students Take Calculus”.

What about angles greater than \(90^\circ\)? Take the angle of \(\theta\) relative to the x-axis, then apply the sign based on which quadrant the angle falls in.

For example, if \(\theta = 135^\circ = 3\pi/4\), then the angle relative to the x-axis is \(\theta' = 45^\circ = \pi/4\). Hence,

(1.12)#\[\begin{align} \sin \theta' &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \cos \theta' &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ \sin \theta &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \cos \theta &= -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{align}\]

1.1.4.2. Physical meaning#

In addition to their geometric significance, trigonometric functions typically represent physical waves.

The properties of the wave can be encoded by manipulating the function.

(1.13)#\[\begin{equation} y = A\sin\left[ \frac{2\pi}{L}(x + x_0) \right] + y_0 \end{equation}\]

where A is the amplitude of the wave, L is the period of the wave, \(x_0\) is a horizontal (phase) shift, and \(y_0\) is a vertical shift.

1.1.5. Identities#

1.1.5.1. Trigonmetric functions#

(1.14)#\[\begin{align} \sin \theta &= \frac{y}{r} & \csc \theta &= \frac{r}{x} = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} \\ \cos \theta &= \frac{x}{r} & \sec \theta &= \frac{r}{x} = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} \\ \tan \theta &= \frac{y}{x} = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} & \cot \theta &= \frac{x}{y} = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta} \end{align}\]
(1.15)#\[\begin{align} \sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1 \\ 1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta \\ 1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta \end{align}\]
(1.16)#\[\begin{align} \cos(A+B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B \\ \sin(A+B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B \end{align}\]
(1.17)#\[\begin{align} \cos 2 \theta = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta \\ \sin 2 \theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta \end{align}\]
(1.18)#\[\begin{align} \cos^2 \theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2 \theta}{2} \\ \sin^2 \theta = \frac{1 - \cos 2 \theta}{2} \end{align}\]
(1.19)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta + 2 \pi) = \sin \theta \\ \cos(\theta + 2 \pi) = \cos \theta \end{align}\]
(1.20)#\[\begin{align} \sin(- \theta) = - \sin \theta \\ \cos(- \theta) = cos \theta \end{align}\]

1.1.5.2. Exponential functions#

(1.21)#\[\begin{align} a^x a^y &= a^{x+y} \\ \frac{a^x}{a^y} &= a^{x-y} \\ (a^x)^y = (a^y)^x &= a^{xy} \\ a^x b^x &= (ab)^x \\ \frac{a^x}{b^x} &= \left (\frac{a}{b} \right)^x \end{align}\]

1.1.5.3. Logarithmic functions#

Definition:

(1.22)#\[\begin{align} y &= \log_{a}x \\ x &= a^y \end{align}\]

Natural Log:

(1.23)#\[\begin{equation} \ln x = \log_{e} x \end{equation}\]

Common log:

(1.24)#\[\begin{equation} \log x = \log_{10} x \end{equation}\]
(1.25)#\[\begin{align} \ln(bx) &= \ln(b) + \ln(x) \\ \ln\left(\frac{b}{x}\right) &= \ln(b) - \ln(x) \\ \ln(x^r) &= r \ln(x) \\ \log_{a}x &= \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)} \\ \end{align}\]
