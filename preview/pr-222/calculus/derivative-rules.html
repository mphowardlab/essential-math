1.4. Rules for derivatives#

1.4.1. Product rule#

The product rule is used to differentiate functions that can be written as the product of two smaller functions.

Product rule

If \(f(x) = u(x) v(x)\), then

(1.49)#\[\begin{align} f'(x) &= u v' + v u ' \\ &= u \dd{}{v}{x} + v \dd{}{u}{x} \end{align}\]

To demonstrate this rule, consider \(f(x) = (x-3)(x-1)\). Previously, we would have needed to expand this function before differentiating:

(1.50)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= x^2 - 4x + 3 \\ f'(x) &= 2x - 4 \end{align}\]

With the product rule, instead we recognize

(1.51)#\[\begin{align} u &= x-3 & v &= x-1 \\ u' &= 1 & v' &= 1 \end{align}\]

so

(1.52)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = uv' + vu' = (x-3) \cdot 1 + (x-1) \cdot 1 = 2x - 4 \end{equation}\]

This example is a little silly, but the product rule is a life saver for functions that are hard to expand!

  1. \(f(x) = (x+1)(2x^2 + 5)(5x^3-4)\)

    Identify:

    (1.53)#\[\begin{equation} u = x+1 \qquad v = (2x^2+5)(5x^3-4) \end{equation}\]

    so

    (1.54)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = (x+1)v' + (2x^2 +3)(5x^3-4)(1) \end{equation}\]

    The derivative \(v'\) can be evaluated by a second product rule!

    (1.55)#\[\begin{align} u &= 2x^2+5 & v &= 5x^3-4 \\ u' &= 4x & v' &= 15x^2 \end{align}\]

    finally giving

    (1.56)#\[\begin{align} f'(x) &= (x+1)[(2x^2+3)(15x^2) + (5x^3-4)(4x)] \\ &+ (2x^2+5)(5x^3-4) \end{align}\]

  2. \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x} e^x\)

    Identify:

    (1.57)#\[\begin{align} u &= \frac{1}{x} & v &= e^x \\ u' &= -\frac{1}{x^2} & v' &= e^x \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.58)#\[\begin{align} f'(x) &= \frac{1}{x}e^x + e^x(-\frac{1}{x^2}) \\ &= e^x\left(\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{x^2}\right) \end{align}\]

  3. \(f(x) = (x^2+3)\ln x\)

    Identify:

    (1.59)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2+3 & v &= \ln(x) \\ u' &= 2x & v' &= \frac{1}{x} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.60)#\[\begin{align} f'(x) &= (x^2+3)\cdot\frac{1}{x} + (\ln x)(2x) \\ &= \frac{x^2+3}{x} + 2x\ln x \end{align}\]

1.4.2. Chain rule#

The chain rule is used to differentiate composite functions, when the “inside” of the function is more complicated than what we know.

Chain rule

If a function \(f(x)\) can be written as \(f(u(x))\), then:

(1.61)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = f'(u) u'(x) \end{equation}\]

To demonstrate this rule, consider \(f(x) = (x-1)^2\). Previously, we would have needed to expand this function before differentiating:

(1.62)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= x^2 - 2x + 1 \\ f'(x) &= 2x - 2 \end{align}\]

With the chain rule, instead we recognize

(1.63)#\[\begin{align} u &= x - 1 & f &= u^2 \\ u' &= 1 & f' &= 2u \end{align}\]

so

(1.64)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = 2u \dd{}{u}{x} = 2(x-1) \cdot 1 = 2x - 2 \end{equation}\]

The results match! Some additional examples:

  1. \(f(x) = e^{x^2}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = x^2\):

    (1.65)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2 & f &= e^u \\ u' &= 2x & f' &= e^u \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.66)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = e^{x^2} \cdot 2x \end{equation}\]

  2. \(f(x) = \ln(1 + 2x)\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.67)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= \ln u \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= \frac{1}{u} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.68)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{1}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{2}{1 + 2x} \end{equation}\]

  3. \(f(x) = \dfrac{2}{1 + 2x}\)

    Make the replacement \(u = 1+2x\):

    (1.69)#\[\begin{align} u &= 1 + 2x & f &= 2u^{-1} \\ u' &= 2 & f' &= -2u^{-2} \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.70)#\[\begin{equation} f'(x) = \dd{}{f}{u} \dd{}{u}{x} = -2u^{-2} \cdot \dd{}{u}{x} = \frac{-4}{(1 + 2x)^2} \end{equation}\]

All of these follow from knowing only two derivatives: \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\)! For example, to find the derivative of \(\tan x\), use the quotient rule:

(1.71)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= \tan x = \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} \\ f'(x) &= \frac{\cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} \\ &= \frac{\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x}{\cos^2 x}\\ &= \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} \\ &= \sec^2 x \end{align}\]

Or, to find the derivative of \(\csc x\), use the chain rule:

(1.72)#\[\begin{align} f(x) &= \csc x = (\sin x)^{-1} \\ f'(x) &= -(\sin x)^{-2} \dd{}{}{x}(\sin x) \\ &= (\sin x)^{-2} \cdot (-\cos x) \\ &= \frac{-1}{\sin x} \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} \\ &= -\csc x \cot x \end{align}\]

1.4.4. Skill builder problems#

  1. \(f(x) = 3 \cos x + \sin x\)

    Solution
    (1.73)#\[\begin{align} f' &= 3 \dd{}{}{x}(\cos x) + 4 \dd{}{}{x}(\sin x)\\ &= -3\sin x + 4\cos x \end{align}\]

  2. \(f(x) = 2 \sin x \cos x\)

    Solution

    Use the product rule with:

    (1.74)#\[\begin{align} u &= \sin x & v &= \cos x\\ u' &= \cos x & v' &= -\sin x \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.75)#\[\begin{align} f' &= 2[\sin x(-\sin x) + \cos x(\cos x)] \\ &= 2(\cos^2 x-\sin^2 x) \\ &= 2 \cos 2x\\ \end{align}\]

  3. \(f(x) = \sin 2x\)

    Solution

    Use the chain rule with

    (1.76)#\[\begin{align} u &= 2x & f &= \sin u \\ u' &= 2 & f' = \cos u \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.77)#\[\begin{equation} f' = \cos{u} \cdot 2 = 2 \cos 2 x \end{equation}\]

  4. \(f(x) = \tan({x^2+2x+3})\)

    Solution

    Use the chain rule with

    (1.78)#\[\begin{align} u &= x^2+2x+3 & f = \tan u\\ u' &= 2x+2 & f' = \sec^2 u\\ \end{align}\]

    so

    (1.79)#\[\begin{align} f' &= \sec^2 u \cdot u'\\ &= (2x+2)\sec^2(x^2+2x+3)\\ \end{align}\]

  5. \(f(x) = e^{\cos^2 x}\)

    Solution

    Use the chain rule with

    (1.80)#\[\begin{align} u &= \cos^2 x & f = e^{u}\\ u' &= \dd{}{}{x}(\cos^2 x) & f' = e^{u} \end{align}\]

    So

    (1.81)#\[\begin{equation} f' = e^{u} u' = e^{\cos^2 x} \dd{}{}{x}(\cos^2 x) \end{equation}\]

    Use the chain rule again with

    (1.82)#\[\begin{align} u &= \cos x & f = u^{2}\\ u' &= -\sin x & f' = 2u\\ \end{align}\]

    So

    (1.83)#\[\begin{align} f' &= e^{\cos^2 x}(2uu')\\ &= e^{\cos^2 x}(2\cos x \cdot -\sin x)\\ &= -2 \sin x \cos x e^{\cos^2 x}\\ \end{align}\]
