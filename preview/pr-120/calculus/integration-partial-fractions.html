1.9. Partial fraction decomposition#

Some ODEs give rise to integrals like:

(1.35)#\[\begin{equation} y' = \frac{3x+11}{x^2-x-6} \to y = \int \frac{3x+11}{x^2-x-6} \, dx \end{equation}\]

This cannot be done with u-substitution… how to take it?

(1.36)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{3x+11}{x^2-x-6} =\frac{3x+11}{(x-3)(x+2)} = \frac{A_1}{x-3} + \frac{A_2}{x+2} \end{equation}\]
(1.37)#\[\begin{align} 3x+11 &= A_1(x+2) + A_2(x-3) \\ &= (A_1 + A_2)x + (2A_1 - 3A_2) \\ \end{align}\]
(1.38)#\[\begin{align} A_1 + A_2 &= 3 \\ 2A_1 - 3A_2 &= 11 \end{align} \]
(1.39)#\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 3\\ 2 & -3 & 11 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 3\\ 0 & -5 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 3\\ 0 & 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 4\\ 0 & 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\]
(1.40)#\[\begin{align} A_1=4 A_2=-1 \end{align}\]
(1.41)#\[\begin{align} y&=\int\left( \frac{4}{x-3}-\frac{1}{x+2} \right)dx \\ &= 4\ln| x-3 | - \ln|x+2| + C \end{align}\]

1.9.1. Partial Fraction Decomposition#

Procedure: For a rational polynomial \( \frac{P\left(x\right)}{Q\left(x\right)}\) where Q is expressed using either linear \(\left(ax+b \right)\) or irreducible quadratic \(\left(ax^2 + bx + c\right)\) factors and the degree of P is less than Q

(1.42)#\[\begin{align} ax+b \to \frac{A}{ax+b} \end{align}\]
(1.43)#\[\begin{align} \left(ax+b \right)^k \to \frac{A_1}{ax+b} + \frac{A_2}{\left(ax+b \right)^2}+ ...+ \frac{A_k}{\left(ax+b \right)^k} \end{align}\]
(1.44)#\[\begin{align} ax^2 + bx + c \to \frac{Ax+B}{ax^2+bx+c} \end{align}\]
(1.45)#\[\begin{align} \left(ax^2 + bx+c \right)^k \text{: repeat like for linear factors} \end{align} \]

Example: Using Partial Fraction Decomposition

To solve

(1.46)#\[\begin{equation} y'= \frac{x^2-29x+5}{\left(x-4 \right)^2 \left(x^2+3 \right)} \end{equation}\]

First, expand the denominator and put placeholder coefficient terms in the numerator.

(1.47)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{x^2-29x+5}{\left(x-4 \right)^2 \left(x^2+3 \right)} = \frac{A_1}{x-4} + \frac{A_2}{(x-4)^2} + \frac{A_3x+ B_3}{x^2+3} \end{equation}\]

Next, cross multiply to eliminate the common denominator.

(1.48)#\[\begin{equation} x^2-29x+5 = A_1 \left(x-4 \right) \left(x^2+3 \right) + A_2 \left(x^2+3 \right)+ \left(A_3x+B_3 \right) \left(x-4 \right)^2 \end{equation}\]

Then, group like powers of x.

(1.49)#\[\begin{equation} =A_1 \left(x^3-4x^2+3x-12 \right)+A_2 \left(x^2+3 \right)+\left(A_3x+B_3 \right)\left(x^2-8x+16 \right) \end{equation}\]
(1.50)#\[\begin{equation} =\left(A_1+A_3\right)x^3+\left(-4A_1+A_2-8A_3+B_3\right)x^2 + \left(3A_1+16A_3-8B_3\right)x+\left(-12A_1+3A_2+16B_3\right) \end{equation}\]

Finally, solve the linear system of equations using Gauss-Jordan Elimination.

(1.51)#\[\begin{align} A_1+A_3 &= 0 \\ -4A_1 + A_2-8A_3 + B_3 &=1 \\ 3A_1 + 16A_3 - 8B_3 &= -29 \\ -12A_1+3A_2+16B_3 &=5 \\ \end{align} \]

Form the augmented matrix then perform elementary operations on rows of this matrix to form pivot points in each row.Then, work back up to turn pivots into 1s and get zeros in each column. The solution comes from reexpressing the augmented matrix as:

(1.52)#\[\begin{equation} \underline(A)\vec(x)=\vec(b) \end{eqution} $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 0\\ -4 & 1 & -8 & 1\\ 3 & 0 & 16 & -8\\ -12 & 3 & 0 & 16 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} A_1 \\ A_2\\ A_3\\ B_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 1\\ -29 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}$ $\to$ $A_1 = 1, A_2 = -5, A_3 = -1, B_3 =2$ \begin{equation} y= \int\left(\frac{1}{x-4}-\frac{5}{\left(x-4\right)^2}-\frac{x}{x^2+3}+\frac{2}{x^2+3}\right) dx \end{equation}\]
(1.53)#\[\begin{equation} \arctan(x)= \int \left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right)dx \end{equation}\]
(1.54)#\[\begin{equation} = \ln|x-4|+\frac{5}{x-4} -\frac{1}{2}ln (x^2 + 3)+\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \arctan\left( \frac{x}{\sqrt{3}} \right) + C \end{equation}\]
