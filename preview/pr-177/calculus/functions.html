1.1. Functions#

1.1.1. Manipulating functions#

Horizontal shift

  • \(f(x+a)\) shifts f left by a.

  • \(f(x-a)\) shifts f right by a.

Vertical shift

  • \(f(x)+b\) shifts f up by b.

  • \(f(x)-b\) shifts f down by b.

Horizontal scaling

  • Scaling \(f(cx)\) compresses f horizontally by c.

  • Scaling \(f(x/c)\) stretches f horizontally by c.

Vertical scaling

  • Scaling \(f(x)/c\) compresses f vertically by c.

  • Scaling \(cf(x)\) streches f vertically by c.

1.1.2. Evaluating and Manipulating Trigonometric Functions#

Each quadrant of the unit circle corresponds to a positive trigonometric function as represented by “A(all)+”, “T(tangent)+”, “S(sine)+”, “C(cosine)+”.

The given unit circle has a triangle drawn within which represents how trigonometric functions are related to lengths and heights of the sides of right traingles. The circle has a radius and is centered at (0,0). A point on the circle is defined by angle \(\theta\) measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis. The trigonometric functions describe how the coordinates of a point on the unit circle relate to the angle \(\theta\).

(1.1)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta)& = \frac{y}{r}& \cos(\theta)& = \frac{x}{r}& \tan(\theta)& = \frac{y}{x}\\ \csc(\theta)& = \frac{r}{y}& \sec(\theta)& = \frac{r}{x}& \cot(\theta)& = \frac{x}{y}\\ \end{align}\]

Angles can be measured in degrees of radians

(1.2)#\[\begin{equation} \theta(rad.) = \theta(deg.)(\frac{\pi}{180^{\circ}}) \end{equation}\]

1.1.2.1. Special Angles#

(1.3)#\[\begin{align} \theta& = 30^{\circ} \frac{\pi}{6}& \theta& = 60^{\circ} \frac{\pi}{3} \end{align}\]

At \( \theta = 30^{\circ} \):

(1.4)#\[\begin{equation} \sin(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \quad \cos(\theta) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \end{equation}\]

At \( \theta = 60^{\circ} \):

(1.5)#\[\begin{equation} \sin(\theta) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \quad \cos(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \end{equation}\]

At \( \theta = 45^{\circ} \)

(1.6)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta)& = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}& \cos(\theta)& = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{align}\]

What about \( \theta \ge 90^{\circ}(\frac{\pi}{2}) \)? Take the angle of \(\theta\) relative to the x-axis, then apply the sign based on which quadrant the angle falls in.

Example: \(\theta = 185^{\circ}\)

The graph shows \(\theta = \frac{3\pi}{4}\) and \(\theta' = \frac{\pi}{4}\) so:

(1.7)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta')& = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}& \cos(\theta')& = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{align}\]

In quadrant two sine is positive so:

(1.8)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta)& = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}& \cos(\theta)& = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{align}\]

Trigonometric functions represent waves. The properties of the wave can be encoded by manipulating the function.

(1.9)#\[\begin{equation} y = A\sin\left[ \frac{2\pi}{L}(x + x_{o}) \right] + y_{o} \end{equation}\]

Where: A = amplitude, \(\frac{2\pi}{L}\) = period, \(x_{0}\) = horizontal shift, and \(y_{o}\) = vertical shift

1.1.3. Identities#

1.1.3.1. Trigonmetric functions#

(1.10)#\[\begin{align} \sin \theta &= \frac{y}{r} & \csc \theta &= \frac{r}{x} = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} \\ \cos \theta &= \frac{x}{r} & \sec \theta &= \frac{r}{x} = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} \\ \tan \theta &= \frac{y}{x} = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} & \cot \theta &= \frac{x}{y} = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta} \end{align}\]
(1.11)#\[\begin{align} \sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1 \\ 1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta \\ 1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta \end{align}\]
(1.12)#\[\begin{align} \cos(A+B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B \\ \sin(A+B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B \end{align}\]
(1.13)#\[\begin{align} \cos 2 \theta = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta \\ \sin 2 \theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta \end{align}\]
(1.14)#\[\begin{align} \cos^2 \theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2 \theta}{2} \\ \sin^2 \theta = \frac{1 - \cos 2 \theta}{2} \end{align}\]
(1.15)#\[\begin{align} \sin(\theta + 2 \pi) = \sin \theta \\ \cos(\theta + 2 \pi) = \cos \theta \end{align}\]
(1.16)#\[\begin{align} \sin(- \theta) = - \sin \theta \\ \cos(- \theta) = cos \theta \end{align}\]

1.1.3.2. Exponential functions#

(1.17)#\[\begin{align} a^x a^y &= a^{x+y} \\ \frac{a^x}{a^y} &= a^{x-y} \\ (a^x)^y = (a^y)^x &= a^{xy} \\ a^x b^x &= (ab)^x \\ \frac{a^x}{b^x} &= \left (\frac{a}{b} \right)^x \end{align}\]

1.1.3.3. Logarithmic functions#

Definition:

(1.18)#\[\begin{align} y &= \log_{a}x \\ x &= a^y \end{align}\]

Natural Log:

(1.19)#\[\begin{equation} \ln x = \log_{e} x \end{equation}\]

Common log:

(1.20)#\[\begin{equation} \log x = \log_{10} x \end{equation}\]
(1.21)#\[\begin{align} \ln(bx) &= \ln(b) + \ln(x) \\ \ln\left(\frac{b}{x}\right) &= \ln(b) - \ln(x) \\ \ln(x^r) &= r \ln(x) \\ \log_{a}x &= \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)} \\ \end{align}\]
